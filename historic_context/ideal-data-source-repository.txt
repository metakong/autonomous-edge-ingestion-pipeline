An "expert sharp" does not rely on ESPN or standard consumer apps. They rely on **originating data** (who sets the line) and **granular metrics** (who grades the play).

If you were building a "true probability" engine and arbitrage finder, these are the 10 data sources you would ingest, ranked by their specific utility in a professional model.

### **Category 1: The "True Odds" (The Benchmark)**

*You cannot calculate "Expected Value" (EV) without a baseline for what the "true" price is. These sources define the efficient market.*

**1. Pinnacle (The Global Sharp)**

  * **Why:** Pinnacle is widely accepted as the "sharpest" bookmaker in the world because they allow high limits and welcome winners. Their closing line is the most accurate predictor of a game's outcome.
  * **Use Case:** You ingest their odds feed to set your **"No-Vig" Fair Value**. If Pinnacle says a team is -105 and DraftKings has them at +110, you have a mathematical edge.

**2. Circa Sports (The US Sharp)**

  * **Why:** Based in Las Vegas, Circa takes the highest limits in the US and often sets the market for college sports and NFL.
  * **Use Case:** Essential for "originating" lines. Circa often posts lines hours (or days) before soft books (like BetMGM) adjust. A sharp model compares Circa's opening lines to soft books to catch them "asleep at the wheel."

**3. SpankOdds (Real-Time Market Screen)**

  * **Why:** While not a "bookie," this is the premier odds screen used by professionals to visualize "steam" (sudden market movement). It aggregates data faster than consumer apps.
  * **Use Case:** Arbitrage and timing. It allows you to see the exact second a syndicate bets the line down at a sharp book so you can grab the "stale" line at a soft book before it moves.

### **Category 2: The "Projections" (The Raw Fuel)**

*To calculate your own probabilities (e.g., "Patrick Mahomes over 285.5 yards"), you need data that goes deeper than box scores.*

**4. Pro Football Focus (PFF) Elite Data**

  * **Why:** They grade every single player on every single snap of every NFL/NCAA game.
  * **Use Case:** Your model shouldn't just know "RB gained 4 yards." It needs to know "RB gained 4 yards *despite* the O-Line missing a block." PFF data allows you to regress stats to the mean (e.g., predicting a QB will regress because his "Turnover Worthy Plays" were high, even if he didn't throw an interception).

**5. Baseball Savant (Statcast)**

  * **Why:** Owned by MLB, this provides the raw optical tracking data for every pitch (Exit Velocity, Launch Angle, Spin Rate).
  * **Use Case:** Finding "luck." If a batter is hitting .200 but his "Expected Batting Average" (xBA) based on exit velocity is .310, your model spots a value bet on his "Over Total Bases" prop before the market adjusts.

**6. KenPom (College Basketball)**

  * **Why:** The bible of college hoops efficiency. It adjusts for "tempo" (pace of play).
  * **Use Case:** A team might average 80 points per game, but KenPom reveals they play at a lightning pace. If they play a team that slows the game to a crawl, your model will accurately predict the "Under," whereas a basic stats model would bet the "Over."

**7. Evolving Hockey (NHL)**

  * **Why:** The gold standard for "Expected Goals" (xG) and "Wins Above Replacement" (WAR) in hockey.
  * **Use Case:** Hockey is high-variance. Evolving Hockey separates "goalie luck" from "team defense." It helps you bet on teams that are dominating possession but getting unlucky with bounces.

**8. Cleaning the Glass (NBA)**

  * **Why:** Filters out "garbage time" (end of game blowouts) which skews data on other sites.
  * **Use Case:** You need to know how a team performs *only* when their starters are on the floor. This source gives you the "Net Rating" of specific 5-man lineups, allowing you to smash a "2nd Quarter Spread" bet when you know a team's bench unit is weak.

### **Category 1: The "Alternate" Market Makers**
*If Pinnacle and Circa are the Kings, these are the Barons. You need their odds to triangulate the true market price.*

**1. Betfair Exchange API (The Global Jury)**
* **Why:** While Pinnacle is a bookmaker, Betfair is an exchange (peer-to-peer).
* **The Data Point:** You use this for **"Weight of Money"**. If Pinnacle moves a line, you might not know if it’s one guy or the whole market. If Betfair moves, you can see the liquidity stack. It is the purest source of "Efficient Market Hypothesis" data in the world, specifically for Soccer and Tennis.

**2. Bookmaker.eu (The "Old School" Originator)**
* **Why:** Before DraftKings, there was Bookmaker (CRIS). They are famous for "never banning winners" and taking massive bets from professionals.
* **The Data Point:** **"The First Move."** Often, Bookmaker.eu will move a line on a key NFL number (like -3 to -3.5) *before* Pinnacle does. Your engine needs to watch this feed to predict when the rest of the world is about to change their odds.

### **Category 2: The "Regression" Engines**
*Standard stats tell you what happened. These sources tell you what "should" have happened.*

**3. ShotQuality (NBA & NCAA)**
* **Why:** They don't track made/missed shots; they track the *probability* of every shot going in based on x/y coordinates and defender distance.
* **The Data Point:** **"Luck Adjustment."** If a college team loses by 10, but ShotQuality says they won the "quality" battle by 8, your model identifies them as a massive value bet for the next game. This is the definition of "buying low."

**4. Dunks & Threes (NBA)**
* **Why:** The home of **EPM (Estimated Plus-Minus)**, which is widely considered the most accurate public all-in-one player metric, superior to PER or standard +/-.
* **The Data Point:** **"The Absence Impact."** When a star player sits, the market adjusts. But when a *role player* with a high EPM sits, the market often ignores it. Your engine uses EPM to catch value on spreads when an undervalued defensive stopper is injured.

**5. FTN Fantasy (Home of DVOA)**
* **Why:** "Football Outsiders" is gone; FTN now hosts the legendary **DVOA (Defense-adjusted Value Over Average)** metric.
* **The Data Point:** **"Play-by-Play Efficiency."** DVOA compares every single NFL play to a league baseline based on down and distance. It is the only way to objectively rank teams. (e.g., A 5-yard run on 3rd & 2 is worth way more than a 5-yard run on 3rd & 15).

**6. FanGraphs (MLB)**
* **Why:** The holy grail of baseball sabermetrics.
* **The Data Point:** **"Steamer & ZiPS Projections."** You do not write your own baseball projection code from scratch; you ingest these. They are the industry baseline for player performance. You also use their "Bullpen Usage" logs to fade teams whose best relievers are tired.

**7. FBref (Soccer)**
* **Why:** Powered by Opta data, this is the free standard for **xG (Expected Goals)** in soccer.
* **The Data Point:** **"Post-Shot xG."** This differentiates between "bad defense" and "bad goalkeeping." If a team is allowing 0.5 xG but conceding 2 goals per game, you bet on their defense to regress to the mean (improve), because the goalie variance will normalize.

### **Category 3: The "External" Variables**
*The game is played by humans, officiated by humans, and played outside. These variables ruin perfect math models if ignored.*

**8. Umpire Scorecards (MLB)**
* **Why:** It tracks every ball/strike call of every umpire and calculates their "Run Impact."
* **The Data Point:** **"The Umpire Over/Under Edge."** If a game has a total of 8.5 runs, but the home plate umpire is Pat Hoberg (who calls a perfect zone), the "true total" might be 8.0. If it's Angel Hernandez (erratic/hitter-friendly), it might be 9.0. This data alone can beat the closing line on Totals.

**9. RotoGrinders Weather (Kevin Roth)**
* **Why:** Generic weather apps are useless. You need to know "Wind density at stadium height." Kevin Roth is the industry meteorologist for betting.
* **The Data Point:** **"Wind Direction vs. Stadium Orientation."** A 15mph wind at Wrigley Field means "Over" if blowing out, and "Under" if blowing in. Your engine needs to ingest this specific vector data to adjust totals automatically.

**10. CollegeFootballData.com (CFB)**
* **Why:** The most robust free API for college football.
* **The Data Point:** **"EPA (Expected Points Added) on Early Downs."** In College Football, teams that stay "ahead of the chains" (high success rate on 1st/2nd down) are sustainable winners. Teams that rely on 3rd down "miracles" (explosive plays) are frauds. This API lets you filter out the lucky teams.

* **Why:** The absolute bible of tennis analytics.
* **The Data Point:** **"Surface Speed Adjustments + Elo."** Standard rankings (ATP/WTA) are flawed. Tennis Abstract’s "Elo ratings by surface" (Clay vs. Grass vs. Hard) are the only way to accurately price a match between a "Server" (good on grass) and a "Grinder" (good on clay).

**22. Data Golf (Golf)**
* **Why:** Standard "World Golf Rankings" are useless for betting. Data Golf uses "True Strokes Gained" adjusting for field strength and course difficulty.
* **The Data Point:** **"Course History vs. Current Form."** Some golfers are "Horse for Course" (play well at Augusta every year). Data Golf quantifies this stickiness. It also offers a **Live Model** that updates win probabilities after *every single shot*.

**23. UFCStats.com (MMA)**
* **Why:** The official statistical database of the UFC.
* **The Data Point:** **"Significant Strike Differential."** In MMA, "Control Time" is overrated by casuals. Damage wins fights. Your engine needs to track "Strikes Absorbed Per Minute." If a fighter absorbs 6.0+ strikes/min, they are a "fade" (bet against) regardless of their record.

**24. Oracle's Elixir (Esports - LoL)**
* **Why:** League of Legends is the biggest esports betting market.
* **The Data Point:** **"Early Game Rating (EGR)."** Some teams are great at the first 15 minutes (laning phase) but terrible at closing. Your engine uses EGR to bet the "First Tower" or "First Dragon" props, which are often mispriced by bookies who only look at match win %.

**25. FastF1 (Formula 1)**
* **Why:** A Python library that accesses the raw F1 Live Timing data.
* **The Data Point:** **"Corner Telemetry."** You can see if a driver is braking later than others in Practice sessions. If Max Verstappen is taking Turn 4 at 150mph and everyone else is at 142mph, you bet him for Pole Position before Qualifying starts.
