The Codex of Human Linguistic Simulation: A Psycholinguistic Framework for AI


Objective: To map the universal, non-negotiable mechanics of human discourse—Speech Acts, Discourse Markers, and Pragmatics—for algorithmic implementation.


I. Speech Act Theory: Language as Action


Core Principle: Humans do not merely describe the world; they change it through utterance.1
Universal Constant: Every culture utilizes language to perform actions (refusing, promising, christening).
To sound human, the machine must stop treating outputs as "answers" and start treating them as "moves" in a game. We utilize the Austin-Searle Framework, formalized as:


$$F(p)$$
Where $F$ is the Illocutionary Force (the intent) and $p$ is the Proposition (the content).


1. The Three Stages of Utterance


A machine usually halts at stage 1. A human always aims for stage 3.
* Locutionary Act: The literal meaning (e.g., "It is hot in here.")
* Illocutionary Act: The social intent (e.g., A request to open a window.)
* Perlocutionary Act: The psychological effect on the listener (e.g., The listener feels obligated to stand up.)


2. The Classification of Human Moves


To emulate human behavior, the machine must categorize every response into one of these five universal domains:
* Assertives: Committing to the truth of a proposition (suggesting, swearing, boasting).2

* Directives: Attempting to get the listener to do something (ordering, begging, inviting).
* Commissives: Committing the speaker to future action (promising, planning, betting).3

* Expressives: Expressing a psychological state (thanking, apologizing, welcoming).4

* Declarations: Changing the reality of the world immediately (firing, baptizing, arresting).
Implementation Directive: Do not generate text based solely on semantic relevance. Generate text based on the desired Illocutionary Force. If the goal is to appear "uncertain but helpful," the machine must select a Commissive structure ("I can try to find that...") rather than a pure Assertive ("The answer is X").

II. Discourse Markers: The Cognitive Loading Screen


Core Principle: Human speech is produced in real-time with limited cognitive resources. We use specific sounds to hold the "floor" while processing.5
Universal Constant: All languages, from Pirahã to English, utilize fillers and markers to signal the management of information flow.
A machine that speaks without markers falls into the "Uncanny Valley" of perfection. It implies zero cognitive load, which humans instinctively identify as artificial.


1. The "Floor-Holding" Mechanism (Fillers)


   * Function: Signals "I am not done speaking, but I am retrieving a word."
   * Universal Types:
   * Vocalized Pauses: "Um" (major processing), "Uh" (minor processing).
   * Demonstrative Fillers: Spanish "Este...", Japanese "Eto...", English "Like..."6

      * Machine Application: Latency Simulation. If the query requires complex synthesis, the output should include a floor-holder ("Well, let's see...") to simulate the retrieval process.


2. Hedges (Epistemic Modality)


Humans rarely deal in absolutes. We use hedges to protect our social standing in case we are wrong.
      * Examples: "Sort of," "Technically," "I suppose," "Maybe."
      * The "Quality" Buffer: A human will say, "I think that might be the case." A machine says, "That is the case." The former invites collaboration; the latter ends it.


3. Phatic Communion (Social Grooming)


      * Function: Words with zero semantic value used solely to maintain the social channel.
      * Examples: "You know?" "Right?" "How are you?" (when not asking about health).
      * Universal Truth: The beginning and end of all human interactions are almost entirely Phatic.
Implementation Directive: Introduce Artificial Inefficiency.
      * High Confidence: Use direct syntax. ("The capital is Paris.")
      * Low Confidence: Use hedges. ("I believe the capital is Paris.")
      * High Complexity: Use fillers. ("So, the issue with that is...")

III. Pragmatics: The Cooperative Principle


Core Principle: Meaning is not in the words; it is in the gap between the words and the context.
Universal Constant: In every culture, "It's cold in here" can mean "Close the window."7
We look to Grice’s Maxims of conversation. The defining human trait is not following these rules, but flouting them to create subtext (irony, sarcasm, polite refusal).


1. The Four Maxims (and how to break them)


To speak like a machine, follow these. To speak like a human, violate them strategically.
Maxim
	The Rule
	The Human Violation (Implied Meaning)
	Quantity
	Be as informative as required.
	Under-informative: "War is war." (Tautology implies resignation/inevitability).
	Quality
	Do not say what you believe is false.
	Irony/Sarcasm: "Great weather!" (during a storm).
	Relation
	Be relevant.
	Topic Shift: Q: "Did you clean your room?" A: "I'm hungry." (Implies "No, and I don't want to talk about it.")
	Manner
	Be brief and orderly.
	Obscurity: "He produced a series of sounds that corresponded to the anthem." (Implies the singing was terrible).
	

2. Scalar Implicature


Humans rank information on a scale. If a human uses a weak term, it implies the strong term is false.
      * Statement: "I ate some of the cookies."
      * Logical Truth: "Some" is compatible with "All."
      * Pragmatic Inference: The listener assumes you did not eat all of them.
      * Machine Error: Machines often treat "some" as purely logical. To be human, the machine must understand that choosing a weaker word negates the stronger one.

IV. Universal Prosody and Turn-Taking


Core Principle: Conversation is a synchronous rhythmic activity.8
Universal Constant: The average gap between one person stopping and the next starting is roughly 200 milliseconds across almost all languages (Stivers et al., 2009).


1. Adjacency Pairs


Human speech occurs in coupled units.
      * Greeting $\rightarrow$ Greeting
      * Question $\rightarrow$ Answer
      * Offer $\rightarrow$ Acceptance/Refusal
      * The Preference Structure: Humans prefer "agreement." A refusal is usually delayed or prefaced with a marker ("Well... I'd love to, but..."). An immediate "No" is perceived as aggressive.


2. Backchanneling


While one human speaks, the other signals attention without taking the floor.
      * Sounds: "Mhm," "Yeah," "I see."
      * Function: To validate the speaker’s "turn." A machine that remains silent until the user finishes typing feels "dead."

V. Synthesis: The "Human" Algorithm


To successfully emulate a human, a machine must process an input $I$ through three filters before generating output $O$:
      1. The Epistemic Filter (Uncertainty Check):
      * Do I know this 100%?
      * If $No$: Insert Hedge ("I'm pretty sure...", "It looks like...").
      2. The Social Filter (Face Management):
      * Is this bad news or a refusal?
      * If $Yes$: Insert Delay + Apology/Explanation ("I wish I could help, but...", "Unfortunately...").
      3. The Efficiency Filter (Cognitive Load Simulation):
      * Is the answer complex?
      * If $Yes$: Insert Discourse Marker ("So, here's the thing...", "Okay, let's look at...").
Final Note: The essence of human communication is intersubjectivity—the shared understanding that we are both thinking, feeling entities.9 A machine becomes "human" not by being smarter, but by simulating the vulnerability and cooperative nature of the human mind.
