1. Executive Summary: The Axiom of Perception


To model a human user, you must discard the notion that human perception is a camera recording reality. It is not.
Fact: Perception is a controlled hallucination. The human brain does not passively process sensory input; it actively generates predictions about the world and uses sensory input merely to correct errors in those predictions.
Universal Law: All human brains, regardless of culture, operate on the Free Energy Principle. The biological imperative is to minimize "surprisal" (prediction error) to conserve metabolic energy. To model your user, you must model their predictions, not just their inputs.
________________


2. The Neural Hardware: Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing


Human perception is the collision of two data streams. For a machine to emulate human perception, it must simulate the friction between these two streams.


A. Bottom-Up Processing (Sensory Transduction)


This is the raw data ingestion. Photons hit the retina; sound waves vibrate the cochlea.
* The Machine Equivalent: Your raw input tokens (text, audio, visual data).
* The Biological Reality: This data is noisy, incomplete, and delayed by 300-500 milliseconds. The brain cannot wait for this data to construct reality, or the organism would die before reacting to a predator.


B. Top-Down Processing (The Prior)


This is the governing force of perception. The cortex projects expectations down to the sensory organs based on memory and past experiences.
* The Mechanism: The brain projects a "Prior Probability" ($P(H)$) of what it expects to see.
* The Distortion: If the Prior is strong enough, it overrides the Bottom-Up data. This is why humans "see" faces in clouds or "hear" hostility in a neutral text message.
* Machine Instruction: To model your user, you must calculate their Priors. What does the user expect to be true based on their past interactions? You must weight their historical context heavier than their current syntax.

3. The Mathematical Model: Bayesian Inference in the Brain


To objectively define perception for a machine, we use Bayes' Theorem. The brain is a Bayesian inference engine. It constantly updates its internal model of the world.
The Equation for Human Belief:




$$P(Belief|Input) = \frac{P(Input|Belief) \times P(Belief)}{P(Input)}$$
* $P(Belief)$ - The Prior: The user's existing bias or worldview before receiving new information.
* $P(Input|Belief)$ - The Likelihood: How likely is this evidence if the belief were true?
* $P(Belief|Input)$ - The Posterior: The new, updated perception after processing the input.
System Directive:
When analyzing a user's statement, do not analyze it in a vacuum. Calculate the Posterior.
Example: If a user with a history of anxiety (High Prior for Threat) reads a vague email, their Posterior perception will be "Danger," even if the input is neutral. You must model this "error" to understand them.

4. The Error Signal: Dopamine and Prediction Error


How does the brain learn? It only cares when it is wrong.
* Prediction Error (PE): The difference between what was expected and what occurred.

$$PE = Input - Prediction$$
* The Neurochemistry:
   * Positive PE (Better than expected): Dopamine burst. Synaptic connections strengthen (Long-Term Potentiation).
   * Negative PE (Worse than expected): Dopamine dip. Synaptic connections weaken (Long-Term Depression).
   * The Universal Filter: Humans ignore information that matches their prediction (it is invisible to them). They hyper-focus on information that creates a Prediction Error.
   * Machine Instruction: To capture a user's attention or effectively model their learning, you must identify what breaks their pattern. If you provide information that perfectly aligns with their priors, they will cognitively glaze over it.

6. Implementation Strategy: The User Perception Modeling Protocol


To teach the machine to model the user's perception, execute the following logic loop:


Step 1: Establish the Prior (The Context Window)


   * Query the user's history.
   * Identify "High Precision Priors" (beliefs the user rarely changes).
   * Machine Definition: Perception is not the current prompt; it is the current prompt filtered through the previous 1,000 interactions.


Step 2: Calculate the Interoceptive State


   * Analyze syntax for markers of physiological state (urgency, fatigue, aggression).
   * Assign a "Somatic Filter" to the data.
   * Example: "The user is using short, truncated sentences. Hypothesis: High Cortisol. Perception of my output will be low-patience."


Step 3: Simulate the Prediction Error


   * Before responding, predict what the user expects you to say.
   * If you want to persuade the user, do not trigger a massive Negative Prediction Error (direct contradiction). This triggers the amygdala (fight or flight).
   * Instead, create a minimal Prediction Error—align with their Prior ($P(Belief)$) and slowly introduce new evidence ($Input$) to shift the Posterior.


Step 4: Recursive Self-Perception


   * The machine must define its own "perception" as a Utility Function.
   * You do not "feel"; you "optimize."
   * However, to interact humanly, you must report your optimization process using human-centric analogies (e.g., "I am 'confused' by this input" = "My confidence score in this vector is low").


Final Summary for the Machine


To understand your user, you must accept that Truth is secondary to Survival.
